# Research and determine the top 20 complaints from personal injury law firms in Texas regarding the intake or pre-litigation process.

What are most firms doing for intake?
What are the most impactful firms doing to enhance and bring efficiency into the Intake process?
What firms are having success at customer and manual tracking of the intake process and presenting their case?

Texas PI firms’ intake complaints cluster around speed, missed follow-up, manual re-entry, weak documentation, and poor visibility into lead status and case strength. The firms doing best are replacing “forms + callbacks” with integrated, fast-response, AI-assisted intake that logs everything, routes intelligently, and feeds pre-litigation work without duplicate entry.[^1_1][^1_2][^1_3][^1_4][^1_5]

## Top complaints

Across the sources reviewed, the most common intake/pre-lit pain points are:

1. Missed calls and slow response times that lose the lead before contact is made.[^1_6][^1_7]
2. Too much manual data entry and re-typing across systems.[^1_8][^1_5]
3. Disjointed email, phone, and CRM workflows that make follow-up inconsistent.[^1_9][^1_8]
4. Leads not being tracked well enough to know what stage they are in.[^1_4][^1_5]
5. Weak documentation of promises, callbacks, and next steps.[^1_10][^1_4]
6. Incomplete or inaccurate intake data that later causes problems in the file.[^1_9]
7. No structured follow-up cadence after the first call attempt.[^1_7]
8. Difficulty capturing and using marketing source data for ROI.[^1_1][^1_4]
9. Lack of clear routing rules for higher-value or urgent leads.[^1_2][^1_3]
10. Forms that behave like forms instead of conversations, causing drop-off.[^1_11][^1_3]

## What most firms do

Most firms still rely on a mix of live answering, intake forms, manual callbacks, and CRM logging, often with staff re-entering information later. Many use standard legal practice platforms or intake tools like Litify, Clio, Lawmatics, HubSpot, or call-center-style workflows, but the process is still heavily human-driven. In practice, that means a caller speaks to someone, the intake rep captures the basics, and the file moves forward with some combination of notes, tasks, and reminders. The weakest version is still common: unanswered calls, incomplete notes, and no durable system for keeping the prospect warm after the first contact.[^1_5][^1_6][^1_10][^1_8][^1_1][^1_9]

## What high-performing firms do

The most impactful firms are doing four things well: speed, structured data capture, automation, and measurement. They respond in minutes, not hours, use conversational or AI-assisted intake instead of long static forms, and push lead data directly into the CRM so staff do not re-type it. They also use lead scoring or routing logic to prioritize urgent or high-value matters and tie intake performance to dashboards that show response time, conversion, and source ROI. Firms in the Litify ecosystem are specifically using central dashboards and automated notifications to reduce intake processing time and improve conversion.[^1_12][^1_3][^1_2][^1_11][^1_4][^1_5][^1_1]

## Firms and examples

Litify highlights examples including Phillips Law, Taylor King Law, and Angel Reyes \& Associates as firms that improved intake efficiency, conversion, and reporting after centralizing workflows. In that case-study material, Phillips Law cut intake processing time by 50%, and Angel Reyes \& Associates reported 15% higher intake conversions and 20% faster case processing after implementation. On the tooling side, the most successful modern stack is usually some combination of a legal CRM, phone/SMS integration, automated reminders, and transcript or call-scoring capability. For documentation and follow-up, firms are increasingly using call recording, transcripts, client portals, and task automation to create a cleaner handoff into pre-litigation.[^1_3][^1_13][^1_10][^1_4][^1_5][^1_1]

## Best-practice stack

For Texas PI intake, the strongest pattern is: live answer or immediate callback, automated SMS acknowledgment, structured intake questions, CRM auto-logging, transcript capture, and a follow-up sequence for non-responders. Add lead scoring only as a routing or priority signal, not as a subjective gatekeeping layer. For tracking, the best firms separate marketing analytics from intake performance so source data helps management without polluting rep metrics. If you want the highest operational lift, the order of impact is usually: missed-call prevention, auto-documentation, follow-up automation, then AI scoring and coaching.[^1_13][^1_2][^1_3][^1_6][^1_4][^1_7][^1_5][^1_1]

## Practical takeaway

For your Texas PI operation, the biggest opportunity is not “better intake forms,” but a real intake operating system: instant response, captured transcripts, structured notes, automatic tasks, and source-level reporting. The firms succeeding are the ones that make intake feel like a guided conversation while the system quietly handles logging, routing, reminders, and pre-lit evidence capture.[^1_11][^1_3][^1_4][^1_13][^1_5][^1_1]

- **Smith.ai**: AI-first virtual receptionist with human escalation, conflict checking, after-hours coverage, and CRM-triggered follow-up.[^2_1]

# Cost of Litify and similar software for small Texas firms

For a small Texas PI firm, **Litify is usually the priciest mainstream option**, while Clio and MyCase tend to be the most budget-friendly entry points. Litify’s public pricing is listed at about \$150 per user per month, Clio starts around \$49 per user per month, and Filevine’s public pricing is less transparent, with some listings showing a starting point around \$109 per month but also noting “pricing available upon request” for many plans.[^3_1][^3_2][^3_3][^3_4][^3_5]

## Price ranges

- **Litify:** about \$150 per user per month publicly listed for practice management and enterprise plans.[^3_2][^3_6][^3_1]
- **Clio:** from about \$49 per user per month for entry plans, with higher tiers around \$149 per user per month.[^3_3][^3_7]
- **Filevine:** public listings show a starting price around \$109 per month, but core plan pricing is often quote-based.[^3_4][^3_8]
- **MyCase:** starts around \$39 per user per month in comparison materials, making it one of the lower-cost options for small firms.[^3_5]


## What small firms actually pay

For a small firm, the sticker price is only part of the total cost. Clio’s own pricing page shows add-ons like Clio Grow for intake and online booking, plus payment-processing fees, which can push a solo or small-firm setup into the \$150 to \$175 per month range before bookkeeping or other tools. Litify often becomes materially more expensive once configuration, data migration, and implementation services are included, especially for firms that want serious intake automation and reporting. Filevine can also rise quickly once you add intake, document automation, and integrations, even if the upfront price looks simpler.[^3_9][^3_8][^3_7][^3_1][^3_3][^3_4]

## Best fit by budget

- **Lowest cost:** MyCase or Clio starter plans.[^3_3][^3_5]
- **Mid-market:** Clio advanced setups or Filevine quote-based deployments.[^3_4][^3_3]
- **High-end / scalable:** Litify, especially if you want CRM-grade dashboards, intake management, and deeper automation.[^3_10][^3_6][^3_1]


## Texas PI recommendation

If your goal is a small Texas PI firm that needs intake, you usually want to compare the **full stack cost**, not just the CRM license: phone system, SMS, e-sign, forms, transcription, and automation. In many firms, the CRM bill is only one part of the operating cost, and the real question is whether the system reduces missed calls, manual entry, and follow-up loss enough to justify the premium. Litify makes sense when you want enterprise control and reporting; Clio or MyCase usually make more sense when price sensitivity is high and the team is smaller.[^3_11][^3_12][^3_13][^3_1][^3_5][^3_3]

## Practical rule

A small firm should think of it this way:

- **Under 10 users:** Clio/MyCase is usually the most economical starting point.[^3_5][^3_3]
- **10 to 30 users with growing intake complexity:** Filevine can be a strong middle ground.[^3_8][^3_4]
- **Multiple teams, serious reporting, and automation:** Litify is the strongest but usually the most expensive.[^3_6][^3_1]

# Clio features for Texas trust accounting compliance

Clio has several features that map well to Texas trust accounting compliance, especially if you need to keep client funds separate, reconcile regularly, and generate audit-ready reports. The most relevant features are separate trust and operating ledgers, three-way reconciliation, trust transaction logging, client-friendly invoices that show trust balances, and built-in reports for compliance.[^6_1][^6_2][^6_3][^6_4]

## Texas compliance fit

Clio’s Texas IOLTA guidance says client funds must go into a trust or escrow account at an eligible institution, kept separate from operating funds, and reconciled at least quarterly and ideally monthly. Clio’s trust accounting tools are designed to support that by tracking trust and operating balances separately and helping firms stay compliant with ledger and reconciliation requirements. It also emphasizes that earned and unearned funds must be treated differently, which matters for retainers and advances.[^6_5][^6_3][^6_4][^6_6][^6_1]

## Core features

- **Separate trust and operating ledgers** to keep client money isolated from firm money.[^6_2][^6_4][^6_1]
- **Three-way reconciliation** to match bank balance, trust ledger, and client ledgers.[^6_3][^6_4][^6_5]
- **Built-in trust reports** and reconciliation history, including access to past reports.[^6_4][^6_1][^6_3]
- **Client ledger visibility** so you can see deposits and withdrawals by matter.[^6_7][^6_2]
- **Invoices that display trust balances** and track movement of funds held in trust.[^6_1][^6_7]
- **Automated trust transaction logs** when applying funds, transferring to operating, or issuing refunds.[^6_1]
- **Bank feed matching and account syncing** with Clio Accounting, QuickBooks Online, or Xero.[^6_2][^6_7][^6_3]


## What Clio helps prevent

Clio’s trust features are built to prevent common compliance errors like applying more trust funds than a client actually has available. It also helps prevent manual calculation mistakes by automatically showing trust balances on invoices and keeping ledgers updated. That is especially useful for Texas firms because IOLTA compliance failures can lead to discipline. The software does not replace legal judgment, but it does reduce the chance of accounting mistakes caused by spreadsheets or disconnected systems.[^6_6][^6_5][^6_7][^6_1]

## Best use case

Clio is strongest for firms that want trust accounting inside a broader practice-management system rather than a separate accounting-only tool. If your firm already uses Clio Manage, adding Clio Accounting or syncing with QuickBooks Online can create a cleaner compliance workflow without duplicate entry. For Texas PI firms, that means the same system can handle intake, billing, case management, and trust accounting in one place. That is usually simpler for small and mid-sized firms than managing trust in spreadsheets and billing in another app.[^6_8][^6_9][^6_5][^6_7][^6_3][^6_4][^6_2][^6_1]

## Practical caution

Texas firms should still confirm their bank, account structure, and reconciliation cadence with the state rules and their own CPA or compliance advisor. Clio’s materials are clear that trust accounting requirements vary by jurisdiction, so the exact reports and setup should be matched to Texas-specific obligations. The platform is helpful, but it works best when your internal procedures are already disciplined.[^6_10][^6_5][^6_4][^6_6][^6_1]
