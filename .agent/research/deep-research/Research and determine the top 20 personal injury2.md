## Lead sources by firm type

Large PI firms usually get leads from Google Ads/LSA, SEO, TV/radio, billboards, call centers, referrals, and repeat brand traffic; smaller firms rely more on referrals, directories, local SEO, and purchased leads because they cannot outspend the incumbents. Best-in-class intake software vendors also emphasize built-in referral networks and automated lead follow-up because conversion speed matters as much as raw lead volume. Litify specifically positions its platform around intake, automation, and referral routing, including a referral network to send cases to other firms.[^1_17][^1_18][^1_3][^1_4][^1_19][^1_5][^1_1]

## Market volumes and costs

I could not verify reliable public lead-volume or cost-by-firm numbers for Texas PI firms, because that data is usually private and changes by campaign, geography, and intake quality. What is public is the size of the broader market: Texas law-firm market revenue is estimated at about \$36 billion in 2026, and Texas PI is one of the most competitive slices of that market. Typical lead-vendor models in the PI space are CPC/CPA, exclusive lead delivery, or call-verified transfer leads, with quality defined by injury status, fault, statute, attorney status, and verification depth.[^1_20][^1_21][^1_22][^1_5][^1_17]

## How smaller firms win

Smaller firms usually break in by picking a narrow lane, like local auto wrecks, trucking, slip-and-fall, or one county/metro, and then building speed-to-lead, review velocity, and local ranking strength. Referral pipelines from other lawyers, medical providers, chiropractors, and lawyer referral services are especially important because they produce higher-trust matters than cold paid traffic. The best-performing smaller-firm mix is usually: local SEO + Google Ads/LSA + lawyer-to-lawyer referrals + medical referrals + a vetted lead vendor for overflow.[^1_23][^1_24][^1_5][^1_1]

## Case trading and drops

There does appear to be a real referral-exchange infrastructure, but it is mostly referral-network based rather than a public “open market” for case contracts. Litify’s referral network is designed to let firms send cases to other firms, monitor status, and manage accountability inside the platform. Outside Litify, the market is usually private: firms trade cases through established referral relationships, co-counsel agreements, or case-by-case handoffs, and they screen hard for reasons like staffing limits, fit, treatment abandonment, venue, damages, or liability issues; I did not find a public commercial marketplace that reliably labels “dropped for staffing” versus “dropped for no treatment” at scale.[^1_25][^1_3][^1_26][^1_4][^1_6]

## Best path in Texas

For a Texas firm trying to enter or expand, the highest-probability path is not buying generic leads alone; it is building a local brand plus a referral engine plus intake automation. If the target is DFW/Houston/Austin auto and truck cases, the best entry wedge is usually high-speed intake, Spanish-language capacity, strong review generation, and a disciplined source-by-source KPI model with paid leads only as a supplemental layer. For case movement, Litify-style referral routing is the cleanest mainstream product I found, but it is a referral system, not a lead marketplace.[^1_10][^1_3][^1_4][^1_19][^1_6][^1_1]

## **Top 10 Business Ideas for a Profitable Case-Trading Marketplace**

### **Model 1: Pay-Per-Signed-Client (Attorney Share Model)**[^2_2]

- **How it works:** Referring attorney posts case for free; accepting attorney pays \$49-\$299 **only when case is signed**
- **Pros:** Aligns incentives, low barrier to entry, no upfront cost for senders
- **Cons:** Requires verification of signature event, refund/dispute mechanism needed
- **Revenue potential:** High volume × moderate fee = scalable SaaS-like revenue
- **Best for:** Smaller firms who can't afford subscription but have overflow cases


### **Model 2: Flat SaaS Subscription (Lexchange Model)**[^2_8]

- **How it works:** Monthly fee (\$99-\$499/month per firm), unlimited sending/receiving
- **Pros:** Predictable recurring revenue, no transaction friction
- **Cons:** Requires critical mass for value, adoption curve slower
- **Revenue potential:** Moderate per-firm, scales with user base
- **Best for:** Mid-size to large firms with steady referral flow


### **Model 3: Freemium + Premium Tiers**

- **How it works:** Free basic marketplace access; paid tiers unlock analytics, priority placement, API integrations, white-label branding
- **Pros:** Maximum adoption, upsell path clear
- **Cons:** Must manage free-tier support costs
- **Revenue potential:** High if conversion rate strong
- **Best for:** Rapid national market penetration


### **Model 4: Transaction Fee (Marketplace Commission Model)**[^2_12]

- **How it works:** Platform takes 5-15% of agreed referral fee (e.g., 33% case fee → platform takes 2-5% of final recovery)
- **Pros:** Revenue scales with case value, no upfront cost
- **Cons:** Requires tracking settlement/verdict outcomes; referral fee transparency; legal/ethics compliance in all jurisdictions
- **Revenue potential:** Very high if large cases traded
- **Best for:** High-value case exchanges (catastrophic injury, mass tort co-counsel)


### **Model 5: Hybrid: Free Send + Success Fee on Accept**

- **How it works:** Sending cases is always free; accepting firms pay success fee only on cases they sign
- **Pros:** Eliminates sender friction, aligns revenue with value delivery
- **Cons:** Complex verification and dispute resolution
- **Revenue potential:** High transaction volume, moderate per-case fee
- **Best for:** Volume-driven marketplace targeting smaller/mid-size firms


### **Model 6: Data + Analytics Upsell**

- **How it works:** Core marketplace free or low-cost; sell premium data products (average case values by type, firm acceptance rates, market trends, geographic heat maps)
- **Pros:** Additional revenue stream, leverages aggregated anonymized data
- **Cons:** Must ensure compliance with privacy/ethics rules
- **Revenue potential:** Moderate add-on revenue for established platform
- **Best for:** Mature marketplace with significant transaction history


### **Model 7: Lead Generation Affiliate (Credit Karma Model)**[^2_13]

- **How it works:** Offer free case-posting; monetize by recommending vetted vendors (IMEs, medical billing, litigation finance, funding companies) and earn affiliate fees
- **Pros:** Multiple revenue streams, no friction for core product
- **Cons:** Must maintain user trust, avoid conflicts of interest
- **Revenue potential:** High if vendor ecosystem strong
- **Best for:** Platform with large active user base


### **Model 8: White-Label Platform Licensing**

- **How it works:** License the case-exchange technology to state bar associations, legal networks, or large firms for internal/co-counsel use
- **Pros:** B2B SaaS revenue, less transaction dependency
- **Cons:** Requires enterprise-grade security and customization
- **Revenue potential:** High contract value, lower volume
- **Best for:** Established platform with proven product-market fit


### **Model 9: Integrated Lead + Case Marketplace**

- **How it works:** Platform sells new leads **and** facilitates case trading/co-counsel matching in one ecosystem
- **Pros:** Captures full lifecycle from lead acquisition to case transfer
- **Cons:** Complex operations, must manage lead quality and case handoff quality separately
- **Revenue potential:** Very high if both sides work well
- **Best for:** Vertically integrated legal marketing + operations platform


### **Model 10: Litigation Finance Embedded Marketplace (LexShares Model)**[^2_14][^2_15]

- **How it works:** Platform not only matches cases but also offers case financing/capital for plaintiffs or firms; revenue from financing fees + case outcome participation
- **Pros:** High-value add, differentiated from pure referral platforms
- **Cons:** Requires capital, regulatory complexity, underwriting expertise
- **Revenue potential:** Very high per-deal, lower volume
- **Best for:** Well-capitalized platform targeting high-value commercial or mass-tort cases

## **Should This Be Part of Lead Gen Platform or Standalone?**

### **Option A: Standalone Marketplace (Recommended for MVP)**

**Pros:**

- Clean focus, faster to build and test
- Appeals to firms who don't need lead generation but do need case exchange
- Easier to integrate with multiple CRMs (Litify, Clio, CASEpeer, Filevine) without vendor lock-in
- Can partner with lead gen vendors rather than compete

**Cons:**

- Must build user base from scratch
- No immediate cross-sell opportunity

**Best for:** Testing product-market fit, proving economics, building credibility before vertical integration

***

### **Option B: Integrated with Lead Gen Platform**

**Pros:**

- Captures full client lifecycle: lead acquisition → intake → case work → case transfer/co-counsel
- Cross-sell opportunity: firms buying leads also have cases to offload
- Data advantage: understand which lead sources produce cases that get traded/dropped and why
- Brand leverage: one login, one ecosystem

**Cons:**

- More complex product, slower to market
- Must maintain quality on two fronts (lead quality + case exchange quality)
- Risk of perceived conflict of interest (selling leads + facilitating case drops)

**Best for:** Established lead gen platform with existing firm relationships looking to add retention/value

## **Litify Integration Strategy: Standardized Data Model**

### **Why Litify-Native Format is a Competitive Advantage**

Litify already has 1000+ daily referrals flowing through its network, and firms using Litify are incentivized to stay within the Salesforce/Litify ecosystem for reporting, compliance, and workflow continuity. If your marketplace **natively exports/imports in Litify field format**, you eliminate data entry friction and create instant value for the largest PI platform user base.[^2_10][^2_9]

### **Core Data Fields for Standardized Case Summary**

Based on Litify's referral architecture  and your intake operating system files, a marketplace-ready case summary should include:[^2_16][^2_17][^2_9]

#### **1. Case Identification**

- Case ID / Matter Number
- Case Name
- Date Opened
- Referral Source (if applicable)
- Current Case Status (Litify status field)


#### **2. Liability \& Coverage**

- Accident Type (auto, truck, slip-and-fall, etc.)
- Date of Incident
- Fault Assessment (clear/disputed/comparative)
- At-Fault Party Name \& Insurance Carrier
- Policy Limits (known/unknown)
- Adverse Party Attorney (if retained)


#### **3. Damages \& Treatment**

- **Injury Type(s):** (soft tissue, fracture, TBI, etc.)
- **Treatment Status:** (active/completed/abandoned)
- **Number of Treatment Visits Completed**
- **Last Treatment Date**
- **Diagnostic Tests Completed:** (X-ray, MRI, CT scan, etc.)
- **Total Medical Bills to Date:** (documented amount)
- **Anticipated Future Treatment:** (yes/no + notes)


#### **4. Documentation Status**

- Police Report Obtained (yes/no)
- Accident Scene Photos (yes/no, count)
- Medical Records Obtained (yes/no, % complete)
- Medical Bills Verified (yes/no)
- Witness Statements (yes/no, count)
- Demand Prepared (yes/no)
- Insurance Adjuster Contact Status


#### **5. Case Economics**

- Estimated Case Value (low/mid/high range)
- Costs Advanced to Date
- Lien Status (Medicare, Medicaid, health insurance, other)
- Statute of Limitations Date


#### **6. Client Information**

- Client Name (anonymized for marketplace browsing, revealed on acceptance)
- Client Contact Status (responsive/unresponsive)
- Client Language Preference
- Signed Retainer Agreement (yes/no)
- Last Client Contact Date


#### **7. Reason for Transfer**

- **Staffing Capacity**
- **Geographic Conflict** (venue outside firm's practice area)
- **Case Type Mismatch** (firm doesn't handle this injury/liability type)
- **Client Non-Compliance** (treatment abandonment, unresponsive)
- **Damages Too Low** (below firm threshold)
- **Liability Dispute** (fault contested, too risky)
- **Coverage Issue** (insufficient policy limits, uninsured motorist issue)
- **Conflict of Interest**
- **Other:** (free text)

# Do any PI firm CRMs categorize case drops by staffing vs no treatment

Yes — **some CRMs can track drop or referral status in ways that approximate staffing vs. no-treatment**, but I did **not** find evidence that the major PI CRMs have a standard, out-of-the-box dropdown specifically labeled “dropped for staffing” and “dropped for no treatment” across the industry. Litify’s referral network clearly tracks referral status changes like pending, declined, and cancelled, but that is a referral workflow, not a universal PI-drop taxonomy.[^3_1][^3_2][^3_3][^3_4]

## What the software actually supports

CASEpeer markets PI-specific fields, medical tracking, and workflow tools, and Filevine is described as highly customizable, which means firms can likely build custom “reason for drop” fields or status pipelines inside them. In practice, that means firms can create internal categories such as “staffing capacity,” “client not treating,” “no-show,” “liability weak,” or “coverage issue,” but those labels are usually **configured by the firm**, not provided as a standard industry taxonomy. Litify’s own referral tools are centered on referral lifecycle status and routing rather than a public claim of PI-specific drop-reason analytics.[^3_2][^3_3][^3_4][^3_1]

## Best interpretation

The closest real-world answer is: **yes, PI firms can categorize this, but only if they build or configure it themselves** inside Litify, Filevine, CASEpeer, or a similar platform. That makes your marketplace idea strong because it can standardize those drop reasons at the point of transfer, instead of relying on each firm’s custom CRM setup.[^3_3][^3_4][^3_1][^3_2]

## Best marketplace design

Your proposed model should use a **required drop-reason taxonomy** with separate fields for:

- Staffing / bandwidth.
- No treatment / abandoned treatment.
- Liability weak.
- Coverage issue.
- Client nonresponsive.
- Venue mismatch.
- Conflict.
- Outside firm appetite.

That structure would create cleaner sorting than what most CRMs appear to offer natively today, and it would make transferred matters easier to evaluate quickly.[^3_1][^3_3]

# What case drop analytics are in Litify or Smart Advocate

Litify and SmartAdvocate both appear to support **status-based workflow tracking**, but I did not find evidence of a standard, built-in “case drop analytics” module that explicitly reports “dropped for staffing” versus “dropped for no treatment” out of the box. What they do support is enough structure for firms to build those analytics themselves through custom statuses, sub-statuses, list views, and reports.[^4_1][^4_2][^4_3][^4_4]

## Litify

Litify’s referral network lets firms track sent referrals and see detailed referral status information, and it explicitly mentions built-in success metrics and referral status monitoring. The public materials I found point to statuses like pending, declined, cancelled, and synchronization states, plus reporting and dashboarding that can be configured around those fields. That means a firm could create drop-reason fields and analytics, but Litify does not appear to publicly advertise a PI-specific “drop reason analytics” dashboard with staffing/no-treatment as standard categories.[^4_5][^4_2][^4_6][^4_4][^4_1]

## SmartAdvocate

SmartAdvocate clearly supports **main statuses** and **sub-statuses**, which is the cleanest public evidence I found for case-stage analytics that can be customized to specific operational reasons. Their documentation gives examples like “Client Still Treating” and “Treatment Completed,” showing that treatment-related status analysis is feasible inside the platform. I did not find public documentation showing a built-in staffing-drop metric, but the sub-status design strongly suggests a firm can create one.[^4_3][^4_7]

## Practical takeaway

If your goal is analytics like “staffing drop vs no-treatment drop,” the likely reality is:

- **Litify:** doable through custom fields, referral statuses, and reports.[^4_2][^4_4][^4_1]
- **SmartAdvocate:** doable through main statuses and sub-statuses, probably even more naturally for matter-stage coding.[^4_7][^4_3]


## Best marketplace fit

This actually strengthens your marketplace concept because you can standardize drop reasons before transfer, instead of relying on each firm’s CRM conventions. A consistent taxonomy and case-summary card would be more valuable than asking every CRM to already have the same analytics baked in.[^4_4][^4_1][^4_2][^4_3]

# Best PI CRM for analyzing drop reasons like no treatment

For **analyzing drop reasons like “no treatment,” Litify is the strongest of the three major PI platforms I found, with SmartAdvocate close behind for status coding flexibility, and Filevine also strong because of its report/sub-status structure. None of them appears to market a ready-made PI dashboard specifically labeled “staffing drop vs no-treatment drop,” so the real advantage is how well each platform lets you build that taxonomy yourself.[^5_1][^5_2][^5_3][^5_4][^5_5]

## Best fit by need

- **Litify:** Best if you want intake, medical chronology, dashboards, and configurable analytics in one PI-focused system.[^5_2][^5_1]
- **SmartAdvocate:** Best if your main goal is deep matter-stage coding through statuses and sub-statuses that can be used for drop analysis.[^5_6][^5_4][^5_7]
- **Filevine:** Best if you want highly configurable reports, sub-status movement, and a flexible workflow engine that can support custom drop analytics.[^5_8][^5_3]


## Why Litify ranks highest

Litify publicly emphasizes PI workflow analytics, case progression tracking, deadlines, medical record requests, and dashboards, which makes it the most naturally aligned with “why did this case stall or drop” analysis. It also has referral-network functionality and reporting around referral status, which helps if you want a drop-to-transfer workflow instead of just an internal case-status report. In plain terms, Litify is strongest when you want the CRM to connect intake, treatment tracking, and reporting in one environment.[^5_9][^5_10][^5_1][^5_2]

## Why SmartAdvocate and Filevine are still useful

SmartAdvocate’s public documentation on sub-statuses suggests you can create detailed operational reasons and then report on them later. Filevine’s help docs show customizable lead/status movement and reporting controls, which is useful if you want to model “no treatment,” “lost contact,” or “staffing overload” as distinct status paths. Those platforms may be slightly less PI-marketing-forward than Litify, but they are still highly workable for custom analytics.[^5_3][^5_4][^5_7][^5_8]

## Practical recommendation

If your priority is **best PI CRM for drop-reason analytics**, I would choose:

1. **Litify** for the best overall PI analytics foundation.[^5_1][^5_2]
2. **SmartAdvocate** if you want status/sub-status precision and a more matter-operations style build.[^5_4][^5_7]
3. **Filevine** if you want maximum configuration and report flexibility.[^5_8][^5_3]

For your marketplace concept, the best move is to standardize drop reasons outside the CRM, then sync them into whichever CRM the firm already uses.[^5_4][^5_9][^5_8]
